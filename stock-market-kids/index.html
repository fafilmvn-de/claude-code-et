What Are Stocks?

Understanding ownership, shares, and why companies go public

🍕

Imagine You Own a Pizza Shop...

You start a pizza shop. It's doing great! But you need $10,000 to open a second location. You don't have that much cash.

So you split your business into 100 equal pieces called "shares". Each share is worth $100. You sell 50 shares to friends and family, raising $5,000. Now they each own a tiny piece of your pizza shop! 🎉

That's exactly what a stock is — a small piece of ownership in a company.

📌 Key Concepts

Stock (or Share): A tiny piece of ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a part-owner!

Stockholder (or Shareholder): Someone who owns stock in a company. That could be you!

Going Public: When a company sells its shares to the public for the first time. This is called an IPO (Initial Public Offering).

🤔 Why Do Companies Sell Stocks?

Companies need money to grow — to build new factories, hire people, create new products, or expand to new countries. Instead of borrowing from a bank, they can sell pieces of themselves (stocks) to raise money.

The trade-off: The company gets money to grow, but now has to share its future success with all the stockholders.

🏷️ Company vs. Stock Price

Here's something important: the stock price is not the same as the company's value.

Think about it: If a company has 1,000 shares at $10 each, the total value is $10,000. But if it has 100 shares at $100 each, the total value is also $10,000!

The price per share depends on how many pieces the company is split into. What matters more is the total value of all shares combined (called "market capitalization").

💡 Did You Know? — The oldest stock exchange ▼
The Amsterdam Stock Exchange, founded in 1602, is considered the world's oldest stock exchange. The Dutch East India Company was the first to sell shares to the public!
💡 Did You Know? — You can own a piece of Apple ▼
Apple has about 15 billion shares. If you buy just 1 share, you technically own a tiny piece of the company that makes iPhones! (Though it's a really, really tiny piece 😄)
💡 Did You Know? — Not all companies are public ▼
Many huge companies like IKEA and LEGO are "private" — they choose not to sell stocks to the public. Only "public" companies have stocks you can buy on the stock market.

The Stock Market

How buyers and sellers meet, and why prices move

🏪

Think of a Farmers' Market...

At a farmers' market, sellers bring their produce and buyers come to shop. If everyone wants strawberries but only one farmer has them, the price goes UP. If no one wants kale, the price goes DOWN.

The stock market works the same way — but instead of food, people are buying and selling shares of companies. The price is set by supply and demand.

📌 What is a Stock Exchange?

A stock exchange is a marketplace where stocks are bought and sold. Famous exchanges include:

🗽 NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) — the largest in the world
💻 NASDAQ — known for tech companies
🇯🇵 Tokyo Stock Exchange — largest in Asia

Today, most trading happens electronically — no shouting on a trading floor needed! 🖥️

📊 Why Do Prices Move?

Stock prices change constantly because of supply and demand:

📈 Price goes UP when more people want to buy (high demand, low supply)

📉 Price goes DOWN when more people want to sell (low demand, high supply)

Things that affect demand include: company news, economic conditions, new products, and even public mood!

💡 Did You Know? — Billions traded daily ▼
On a typical day, billions of dollars worth of stocks change hands on the NYSE alone. The computers that match buyers and sellers can process thousands of trades per second!

Understanding Companies

What makes a company valuable — revenue, profit, and more

🍋

The Lemonade Stand

Imagine you run a lemonade stand. You sell $50 worth of lemonade in a day — that's your revenue (total money coming in).

But you spent $30 on lemons, sugar, and cups. So your profit is only $20 (what's left after expenses).

Revenue ≠ Profit! A company can have huge revenue but tiny profit if it spends a lot. Smart investors look at both.

🏆 What Makes a Company "Good"?

When evaluating a company, think about:

💰 Revenue Growth — Is the company earning more money each year?

📈 Profit — Is it actually keeping money after paying costs?

💝 Brand — Do people know and trust the company?

🛍️ Products — Do customers love what it sells?

🔄 Repeat Customers — Do people keep coming back?

Intro to Stock Evaluation

Long-term thinking and what makes a stock worth buying

🌳

Investing is Like Planting a Tree

When you plant a tree, you don't dig it up every day to check the roots. You water it, give it sunlight, and wait patiently.

Long-term investing works the same way. You pick a good company, buy its stock, and let it grow over time. Checking the price every minute won't make it grow faster! 😄

⏰ Long-Term vs. Short-Term

🌳 Long-Term Investor

Buys good companies and holds for years. Focuses on company quality. Less stressful. Most successful investors do this!

⚡ Short-Term Trader

Buys and sells within days or hours. Tries to predict price changes. Very risky. Most short-term traders lose money.

📌 For beginners, long-term investing is the way to go. Even legendary investor Warren Buffett says his favorite holding period is "forever"!

🔍 Simple Things to Look For

You don't need complicated math. Here are simple signs a company might be a good long-term investment:

✅ Growing revenue — The company is earning more each year

✅ Consistent profits — It's not just growing, it's also keeping money

✅ Strong brand — People know and trust the name

✅ Good products/services — Customers keep coming back

✅ Good leadership — Smart people running the company

⛔ Warning signs: Shrinking revenue, losing money every year, lots of debt, customers leaving

How Trading Works

Buying, selling, gains, and losses — step by step

🛒 What Happens When You Buy a Stock?

Here's the step-by-step process:

1️⃣
You decide to buy

You choose a company and decide how many shares you want.

2️⃣
Your order goes to the exchange

The stock exchange matches your buy order with someone who wants to sell.

3️⃣
Money is exchanged for shares

Your money goes to the seller, and the shares appear in your account. All electronic!

4️⃣
You own part of the company!

The shares are now yours. Their value will go up or down with the company's stock price.

💰 Understanding Gains & Losses

Gain: If you buy at $20 and the stock goes to $30, you've gained $10 per share. That's a 50% gain! 🎉

Loss: If you buy at $20 and the stock drops to $15, you've lost $5 per share. That's a 25% loss. 😢

Important: You only actually gain or lose money when you sell. If you don't sell, it's called an "unrealized" gain or loss — it's on paper only.
