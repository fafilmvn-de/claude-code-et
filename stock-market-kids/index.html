What Are Stocks?

Understanding ownership, shares, and why companies go public

🍕

Imagine You Own a Pizza Shop...

You start a pizza shop. It's doing great! But you need $10,000 to open a second location. You don't have that much cash.

So you split your business into 100 equal pieces called "shares". Each share is worth $100. You sell 50 shares to friends and family, raising $5,000. Now they each own a tiny piece of your pizza shop! 🎉

That's exactly what a stock is — a small piece of ownership in a company.

📌 Key Concepts

Stock (or Share): A tiny piece of ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a part-owner!

Stockholder (or Shareholder): Someone who owns stock in a company. That could be you!

Going Public: When a company sells its shares to the public for the first time. This is called an IPO (Initial Public Offering).

🤔 Why Do Companies Sell Stocks?

Companies need money to grow — to build new factories, hire people, create new products, or expand to new countries. Instead of borrowing from a bank, they can sell pieces of themselves (stocks) to raise money.

The trade-off: The company gets money to grow, but now has to share its future success with all the stockholders.

🏷️ Company vs. Stock Price

Here's something important: the stock price is not the same as the company's value.

Think about it: If a company has 1,000 shares at $10 each, the total value is $10,000. But if it has 100 shares at $100 each, the total value is also $10,000!

The price per share depends on how many pieces the company is split into. What matters more is the total value of all shares combined (called "market capitalization").

💡 Did You Know? — The oldest stock exchange ▼
The Amsterdam Stock Exchange, founded in 1602, is considered the world's oldest stock exchange. The Dutch East India Company was the first to sell shares to the public!
💡 Did You Know? — You can own a piece of Apple ▼
Apple has about 15 billion shares. If you buy just 1 share, you technically own a tiny piece of the company that makes iPhones! (Though it's a really, really tiny piece 😄)
💡 Did You Know? — Not all companies are public ▼
Many huge companies like IKEA and LEGO are "private" — they choose not to sell stocks to the public. Only "public" companies have stocks you can buy on the stock market.

⭐⭐ Medium Market Capitalisation

Market cap is the total value the stock market places on a company: share price × total shares outstanding.

If a company has 100 million shares trading at $50 each, its market cap is $5 billion. Size categories: Large-cap (>$10B), Mid-cap ($2B–$10B), Small-cap (<$2B).

💡 A $5 stock can belong to a company worth more than a $500 stock — market cap matters more than share price alone.

⭐⭐ Medium Dividends

Some companies share a portion of their profits with shareholders as dividends — regular cash payments per share, usually paid quarterly.

Example: You own 200 shares and the company pays a $0.50 quarterly dividend → you receive $100 per quarter ($400/year) just for holding the stock.

Not all companies pay dividends — many reinvest all profits to grow faster. Mature, stable companies (banks, utilities) tend to be the biggest payers.

⭐⭐ Medium 💡 Did You Know? — Market cap milestone ▼
Apple first touched a $3 trillion market cap intraday in January 2022, but only crossed it sustainably in June 2023 — making it the first company ever to do so. It got there by having billions of shares outstanding at a high share price, not just by having an expensive stock.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Share Dilution

When a company issues new shares to raise money, existing shareholders own a smaller percentage — this is called dilution.

Example: You own 1,000 of 10,000 total shares (10%). The company issues 10,000 more shares. Now you own 1,000 of 20,000 — only 5%. Your slice of the pie shrank even though you did nothing.

⚠️ Dilution isn't always bad — if the new capital fuels big growth, each smaller slice can still become more valuable. Watch out for companies that dilute repeatedly without showing growth.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Stocks vs Bonds

Stocks = ownership. You share in the company's profits and losses.

Bonds = a loan to the company (or government). The issuer promises to pay you interest and return your principal. Bondholders are creditors — they get paid before stockholders if a company goes bankrupt.

📊 Stocks offer higher potential returns but more risk. Bonds are generally safer but lower return. Most adult investors hold a mix of both to balance risk.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard IPO Lock-Up Period

When a company goes public (IPO), insiders — founders, early employees, and early investors — are typically barred from selling shares for 90 to 180 days.

Why? To prevent insiders from immediately dumping their shares and crashing the price right as public investors are buying in.

📅 When the lock-up expires, watch out: if insiders sell heavily, the price can drop sharply. Tracking lock-up expiry dates is part of a serious investor's toolkit.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard 💡 Did You Know? — The Facebook IPO lock-up crash ▼
When Facebook went public in May 2012, insiders held hundreds of millions of shares. Three months later, when the first lock-up period expired, insiders sold heavily — and the stock dropped another 50% from its already-struggling IPO price. It took over a year to recover. Lock-up expirations are serious events that smart investors track carefully.

The Stock Market

How buyers and sellers meet, and why prices move

🏪

Think of a Farmers' Market...

At a farmers' market, sellers bring their produce and buyers come to shop. If everyone wants strawberries but only one farmer has them, the price goes UP. If no one wants kale, the price goes DOWN.

The stock market works the same way — but instead of food, people are buying and selling shares of companies. The price is set by supply and demand.

📌 What is a Stock Exchange?

A stock exchange is a marketplace where stocks are bought and sold. Famous exchanges include:

🗽 NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) — the largest in the world
💻 NASDAQ — known for tech companies
🇯🇵 Tokyo Stock Exchange — largest in Asia

Today, most trading happens electronically — no shouting on a trading floor needed! 🖥️

📊 Why Do Prices Move?

Stock prices change constantly because of supply and demand:

📈 Price goes UP when more people want to buy (high demand, low supply)

📉 Price goes DOWN when more people want to sell (low demand, high supply)

Things that affect demand include: company news, economic conditions, new products, and even public mood!

💡 Did You Know? — Billions traded daily ▼
On a typical day, billions of dollars worth of stocks change hands on the NYSE alone. The computers that match buyers and sellers can process thousands of trades per second!

⭐⭐ Medium The Bid-Ask Spread

Every stock has two prices simultaneously:

• Bid — the highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay
• Ask — the lowest price a seller will currently accept

The spread is the gap between them. Example: Bid $49.90, Ask $50.10 → spread = $0.20. Market makers earn money by sitting in the middle of this gap.

Popular, liquid stocks (Apple, Google) have tiny spreads. Rarely-traded stocks have wide spreads — a hidden cost for traders. 💸

⭐⭐ Medium How News Moves Prices

Stock prices are driven by expectations. When news changes what investors expect about a company's future, prices adjust immediately — sometimes within milliseconds.

📈 Good earnings report → investors want to own the stock → more buyers → price rises
📉 Bad news or scandal → investors want out → more sellers → price falls

Tricky part: a stock can fall even on good news, if the news was slightly below what investors expected. "Beat expectations by 5%? We expected 10%!" — and the price drops anyway.

⭐⭐ Medium After-Hours Trading

Most major exchanges operate during set hours (e.g., NYSE: 9:30am–4:00pm ET). But many brokers allow trading before and after these hours via electronic networks.

Pre-market: 4am–9:30am ET  |  After-hours: 4pm–8pm ET

⚠️ Extended-hours trading has lower volume, so spreads are wider and prices can move more violently. Major announcements (like earnings releases) often drop after market close, causing dramatic after-hours swings that set the tone for the next morning.

⭐⭐ Medium 💡 Did You Know? — Earnings season ▼
Four times a year, most public companies report their quarterly earnings. This period is called "earnings season." Stock prices can swing 10–20% overnight based on whether results beat or miss expectations — even on fundamentally healthy companies.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Circuit Breakers

To prevent panic-driven crashes, exchanges use circuit breakers — automatic rules that pause trading when markets fall too far, too fast.

US markets (based on S&P 500 moves from prior close):
• Level 1: −7% → 15-minute trading halt
• Level 2: −13% → 15-minute trading halt
• Level 3: −20% → trading halted for the rest of the day

🛑 Goal: give investors time to think calmly instead of panic-selling. Circuit breakers were introduced after "Black Monday" (October 19, 1987) when the Dow Jones fell 22.6% in a single day.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Short Selling

Short selling is a bet that a stock's price will fall.

How it works: 1) Borrow shares from your broker → 2) Sell them at the current price → 3) Wait for the price to drop → 4) Buy the shares back cheaper → 5) Return the borrowed shares and pocket the difference.

Example: Borrow and sell 100 shares at $80. Price falls to $50. Buy back at $50. Profit: ($80 − $50) × 100 = $3,000.

⚠️ Unlimited risk: If the price rises instead of falls, losses have no ceiling — a stock can theoretically rise forever. This makes short selling one of the riskiest strategies in investing. 🔥

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Stock Market Indices

A stock market index tracks the performance of a selected group of stocks to measure how the overall market (or a sector) is doing.

Famous indices:
• S&P 500 — 500 largest US companies
• Dow Jones (DJIA) — 30 major US blue-chip companies
• Nasdaq Composite — heavy tech weighting
• Nikkei 225 — top 225 Japanese companies

📊 When people say "the market was up 2% today," they mean an index. Index funds let ordinary investors own a tiny slice of every company in an index — simple, cheap, diversified investing.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard 💡 Did You Know? — Black Monday 1987 ▼
On October 19, 1987 ("Black Monday"), the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 22.6% in a single day — the largest one-day percentage drop in stock market history. No single cause was ever identified; computer-driven "program trading" that auto-sold when prices fell is often cited as a major amplifier.

Understanding Companies

What makes a company valuable — revenue, profit, and more

🍋

The Lemonade Stand

Imagine you run a lemonade stand. You sell $50 worth of lemonade in a day — that's your revenue (total money coming in).

But you spent $30 on lemons, sugar, and cups. So your profit is only $20 (what's left after expenses).

Revenue ≠ Profit! A company can have huge revenue but tiny profit if it spends a lot. Smart investors look at both.

🏆 What Makes a Company "Good"?

When evaluating a company, think about:

💰 Revenue Growth — Is the company earning more money each year?

📈 Profit — Is it actually keeping money after paying costs?

💝 Brand — Do people know and trust the company?

🛍️ Products — Do customers love what it sells?

🔄 Repeat Customers — Do people keep coming back?

⭐⭐ Medium The P/E Ratio

The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio tells you how much investors pay for every $1 of a company's annual earnings.

Formula: P/E = Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)

Example: Stock price $60, EPS $3 → P/E = 20. Investors are willing to pay $20 for each $1 of earnings.

A high P/E often signals investors expect fast future growth. A low P/E can mean a bargain — or hidden problems. Always compare P/E to industry peers, not in isolation. 🔢

⭐⭐ Medium Gross Profit Margin

Gross profit margin shows how much revenue a company keeps after paying the direct costs of making its products.

Formula: Gross Margin = (Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold) ÷ Revenue × 100%

Example: Revenue $10M, COGS $4M → Gross Margin = 60%. The company keeps $0.60 of every dollar earned after direct production costs.

Software companies often have 70–80% margins; supermarkets might manage 20–25%. Higher margins generally mean more pricing power or operational efficiency. 📐

⭐⭐ Medium The Balance Sheet

A balance sheet is a financial snapshot showing what a company owns and what it owes at a specific point in time.

• Assets: everything owned (cash, inventory, property, patents)
• Liabilities: everything owed (loans, unpaid bills, bonds)
• Equity: Assets − Liabilities = the owners' share

The name comes from the core equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Both sides always balance. A strong balance sheet has more assets than liabilities and healthy cash. 📸

⭐⭐ Medium 💡 Did You Know? — Buffett's P/E preference ▼
Warren Buffett generally prefers buying companies with P/E ratios that seem "reasonable" for their earnings power — not sky-high growth multiples. Yet he held Apple, which often traded at a P/E above 30, because he valued its ecosystem, brand loyalty, and enormous buyback program as forms of hidden earnings.

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Growth Investing & Loss-Making Companies

Many hugely successful companies — Amazon, Uber, Spotify — lost money for years while growing rapidly, yet investors poured money in. Why?

Growth investors bet that a company is intentionally spending more than it earns to capture market share quickly. Once dominant, it can flip to profitability. A company losing $50M/year but growing 100% annually may be far more valuable than a profitable company growing 5%/year.

⚠️ Not all loss-making "growth" companies ever turn profitable. Always ask: what is the realistic path to profitability, and how much runway (cash) does the company have? 🚀

⭐⭐⭐ Hard Return on Equity (ROE)

ROE measures how effectively a company turns shareholders' capital into profit.

Formula: ROE = Net Profit ÷ Shareholders' Equity × 100%

Example: Net profit $5M, shareholders' equity $25M → ROE = 20%. For every $100 shareholders have invested, the company generates $20 in annual profit.

Higher ROE = more efficient capital use. Warren Buffett looks for companies with consistently high ROE (15%+). ⚠️ Watch out: a high ROE achieved through heavy debt isn't the same as genuine efficiency — always check the balance sheet alongside ROE. 💡

⭐⭐⭐ Hard The Competitive Moat

A competitive moat is a durable advantage that protects a company from competitors — like the water-filled trench around a medieval castle. 🏰

Types of moats:
• Brand: Customers trust and prefer it (Coca-Cola, Nike)
• Network effects: Gets more valuable as more people use it (Visa, social platforms)
• Switching costs: Too expensive or painful to change providers (enterprise software)
• Cost advantage: Can produce more cheaply than competitors (Amazon logistics)

Companies with wide moats can defend their profits for decades. No moat = competitors eat your lunch eventually. 🍱

⭐⭐⭐ Hard 💡 Did You Know? — Amazon's 20-year journey ▼
Amazon was founded in 1994 and went public in 1997 at $18/share. It lost money for most of its first 20 years, yet its stock rose thousands of percent. Investors who understood Jeff Bezos's deliberate strategy of reinvesting all profits into growth were rewarded. Those who saw only "unprofitable company" and avoided it missed one of the greatest investments in history.

Intro to Stock Evaluation

Long-term thinking and what makes a stock worth buying

🌳

Investing is Like Planting a Tree

When you plant a tree, you don't dig it up every day to check the roots. You water it, give it sunlight, and wait patiently.

Long-term investing works the same way. You pick a good company, buy its stock, and let it grow over time. Checking the price every minute won't make it grow faster! 😄

⏰ Long-Term vs. Short-Term

🌳 Long-Term Investor

Buys good companies and holds for years. Focuses on company quality. Less stressful. Most successful investors do this!

⚡ Short-Term Trader

Buys and sells within days or hours. Tries to predict price changes. Very risky. Most short-term traders lose money.

📌 For beginners, long-term investing is the way to go. Even legendary investor Warren Buffett says his favorite holding period is "forever"!

🔍 Simple Things to Look For

You don't need complicated math. Here are simple signs a company might be a good long-term investment:

✅ Growing revenue — The company is earning more each year

✅ Consistent profits — It's not just growing, it's also keeping money

✅ Strong brand — People know and trust the name

✅ Good products/services — Customers keep coming back

✅ Good leadership — Smart people running the company

⛔ Warning signs: Shrinking revenue, losing money every year, lots of debt, customers leaving

How Trading Works

Buying, selling, gains, and losses — step by step

🛒 What Happens When You Buy a Stock?

Here's the step-by-step process:

1️⃣
You decide to buy

You choose a company and decide how many shares you want.

2️⃣
Your order goes to the exchange

The stock exchange matches your buy order with someone who wants to sell.

3️⃣
Money is exchanged for shares

Your money goes to the seller, and the shares appear in your account. All electronic!

4️⃣
You own part of the company!

The shares are now yours. Their value will go up or down with the company's stock price.

💰 Understanding Gains & Losses

Gain: If you buy at $20 and the stock goes to $30, you've gained $10 per share. That's a 50% gain! 🎉

Loss: If you buy at $20 and the stock drops to $15, you've lost $5 per share. That's a 25% loss. 😢

Important: You only actually gain or lose money when you sell. If you don't sell, it's called an "unrealized" gain or loss — it's on paper only.
