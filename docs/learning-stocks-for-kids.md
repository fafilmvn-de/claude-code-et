## Module 1: What Are Stocks? 🍕

### The Pizza Shop Analogy

Imagine you start a pizza shop. It's doing great, but you need **$10,000** to open a second location. You don't have that much cash.

So you split your business into **100 equal pieces** called **"shares."** Each share is worth **$100**. You sell 50 shares to friends and family, raising $5,000. Now they each own a tiny piece of your pizza shop!

**That's what a stock is — a small piece of ownership in a company.**

### Key Concepts

- **Stock (Share):** A tiny piece of ownership in a company. Buy one stock and you become a part-owner.
- **Stockholder (Shareholder):** Someone who owns stock. That could be you!
- **Going Public (IPO):** When a company sells shares to the public for the first time.

### Why Do Companies Sell Stocks?

Companies need money to grow — to build factories, hire people, or create new products. Instead of borrowing from a bank, they can sell pieces of themselves (stocks) to raise money.

**The trade-off:** The company gets money to grow, but shares its future success with stockholders.

### Company vs. Stock Price

The stock price is NOT the same as the company's value:
- 1,000 shares × $10 each = $10,000 total value
- 100 shares × $100 each = also $10,000 total value

What matters is the **total value** of all shares (called "market capitalization"), not just the price of one share.

### 💡 Did You Know?

- The Amsterdam Stock Exchange (founded 1602) is the world's oldest stock exchange.
- Apple has about 15 billion shares — buy 1 and you technically own a piece of the company!
- Companies like IKEA and LEGO are "private" — they don't sell stocks to the public.

### ✏️ Exercise

**Question:** If a lemonade stand is worth $200 and splits into 20 shares, what's each share worth?

**Answer:** $200 ÷ 20 = **$10 per share**

## Module 2: The Stock Market 📈

### The Farmers' Market Analogy

At a farmers' market, sellers bring produce and buyers come to shop. If everyone wants strawberries but only one farmer has them, the price goes UP. If nobody wants kale, the price goes DOWN.

The stock market works the same way, but people buy and sell **shares of companies** instead of food.

### What is a Stock Exchange?

A stock exchange is a **marketplace** where stocks are bought and sold:

- 🗽 **NYSE** (New York Stock Exchange) — the largest in the world
- 💻 **NASDAQ** — known for tech companies
- 🇯🇵 **Tokyo Stock Exchange** — largest in Asia

Today, most trading happens electronically — no need for shouting on a trading floor!

### Why Do Prices Move?

Stock prices change because of **supply and demand:**

- 📈 **Price goes UP** when more people want to buy (high demand, low supply)
- 📉 **Price goes DOWN** when more people want to sell (low demand, high supply)

Things that affect demand: company news, economic conditions, new products, and public sentiment.

### ✏️ Exercise

**Scenario:** A popular phone company just announced an amazing new phone. What do you think happens to their stock price? Why?

**Think about it:** More people probably want to buy the stock (demand up) → price goes up!

## Module 3: Understanding Companies 🏪

### Revenue vs. Profit

- **Revenue** = Total money coming in (how much you sell)
- **Profit** = Revenue minus expenses (what's left after paying costs)

**Example:** A lemonade stand earns $50 (revenue) but spends $30 on supplies. Profit = $50 - $30 = **$20**.

A company can have huge revenue but tiny profit if it spends too much!

### What Makes a Company Valuable?

When evaluating a company, think about:

1. 💰 **Revenue Growth** — Is the company earning more each year?
2. 📈 **Profit** — Is it keeping money after paying costs?
3. 💝 **Brand** — Do people know and trust the name?
4. 🛍️ **Products** — Do customers love what it sells?
5. 🔄 **Repeat Customers** — Do people keep coming back?

## Module 4: Intro to Stock Evaluation 💡

### Investing is Like Planting a Tree 🌳

When you plant a tree, you don't dig it up every day to check the roots. You water it, give it sunlight, and **wait patiently**.

Long-term investing works the same way — pick a good company, buy its stock, and let it grow over time.

### Long-Term vs. Short-Term

| Aspect | 🌳 Long-Term Investor | ⚡ Short-Term Trader |
| ------ | --------------------- | -------------------- |
| Time | Holds for years | Buys/sells in days or hours |
| Focus | Company quality | Price predictions |
| Stress | Lower | Very high |
| Success rate | Higher for most people | Most traders lose money |
| Example | Warren Buffett | Day traders |

**For beginners, long-term investing is the way to go.** Warren Buffett says his favorite holding period is "forever"!

### Simple Things to Look For

✅ Growing revenue year over year
✅ Consistent profits
✅ Strong, trusted brand
✅ Products customers love
✅ Good leadership

⛔ Warning signs: Shrinking revenue, yearly losses, heavy debt, customers leaving

### ✏️ Exercise: Spot the Better Investment

**Company A — SteadyMart:**
- Revenue: $500M → $550M → $600M (steady growth)
- Profitable every year for 10 years
- Well-known brand, loyal customers

**Company B — HypeTech:**
- Revenue: $50M → $200M → $180M (volatile)
- Lost money 2 of last 3 years
- Trending on social media this week

**Which would you choose for a 5-year investment?** (Think about stability, consistency, and long-term potential.)

## Module 5: How Trading Works 🛒

### Step-by-Step: Buying a Stock

1. **You decide to buy** — Choose a company and number of shares
2. **Your order goes to the exchange** — Matched with a seller
3. **Money is exchanged for shares** — Your money goes to the seller, shares appear in your account
4. **You own part of the company!** — The shares' value moves with the stock price

### Understanding Gains & Losses

- **Gain:** Buy at $20, price rises to $30 → **+$10 per share (50% gain)** 🎉
- **Loss:** Buy at $20, price drops to $15 → **-$5 per share (25% loss)** 📉

**Important:** You only actually gain or lose money when you **sell**. Until then, it's an "unrealized" gain or loss.

### Key Vocabulary

- **Order** — An instruction to buy or sell a stock
- **Portfolio** — All the stocks you own, collectively
- **Diversification** — Spreading money across different stocks to reduce risk
- **Liquidity** — How easily a stock can be bought or sold

### ✏️ Exercise

**Scenario:** You bought 5 shares of a company at $30 each (total investment: $150). The stock price drops to $25.

1. What is your current loss per share? (**$5**)
2. What is your total unrealized loss? (**$25**)
3. Have you actually lost money yet? (**No — only if you sell!**)
4. If the company is still strong, what might a long-term investor do? (**Hold and wait for recovery**)

## Module 6: Practice Zone 🎮

### What is Paper Trading?

**Paper trading** means practicing trades with fake money. No real money is involved — it's a safe way to learn before ever considering real investing.

### The Simulator

The interactive web version includes a full paper trading simulator:

- **$10,000** starting cash (fake money!)
- **5 fictional companies** to trade
- **8 simulated weeks** with random news events
- Buy and sell shares, track your portfolio
- See how news affects stock prices

### Reflection Questions

After completing a simulation, think about:

1. What strategy did you use? Did it work?
2. Did you diversify (spread your money) or concentrate?
3. How did news events affect your companies?
4. What would you do differently next time?
5. Did any price drops make you want to panic-sell?

### Key Takeaways

- 📚 Investing is about **understanding companies**, not gambling
- 🌳 **Patience** is the most important skill
- 🎲 **Diversification** reduces risk — don't put all eggs in one basket
- 📰 **News** affects prices, but not always permanently
- 🧠 **Emotions** can lead to bad decisions — think, don't react
